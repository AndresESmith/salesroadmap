Block 01 · Days 1–7

The Knowledge Base

The exact mechanics of a web-design cold call. 2 hours of study + list-building, 1 hour memorizing the script out loud. No dialing yet.

ABuild the target list (Days 1–2)

The best prospect is a business that (a) depends on local customers finding them and (b) has no website, an ugly/broken one, or one that isn't mobile-friendly.

Hunt these verticals: home services (HVAC, plumbing, roofing, electrical, landscaping), restaurants, dentists & medical, real estate, gyms & studios, salons & spas, auto shops, attorneys.

Google "[service] near [your town]" and nearby towns, open each Google Business Profile, and flag the ones with no website link, a Facebook page used as a website, or a 2012-era site that breaks on your phone. Pull: business name, owner's first name (from the profile, reviews, or "About" page), phone, and the one specific problem you spotted.

Target

50 qualified prospects, each with a real flaw noted, before you ever pick up the phone.

BBypassing the gatekeeper

For true SMBs the owner often answers directly — this is a smaller wall than people fear. When there is a gatekeeper:

  • Sound like a peer, not a vendor. Lower your energy, slow down, use the first name: "Hey, is Mike around?" Salespeople over-explain; insiders don't.
  • Assume the transfer, don't ask permission. "Can you put me through to Mike?" beats "Is it possible to maybe speak with whoever handles the website?"
  • If asked "what's this regarding?" — be honest but low-stakes: "It's about his Google listing — I'll only need him a sec." Never say "I'm calling to sell," never lie.
  • Timing beats tactics. Call before 8:30am or after 5pm, when gatekeepers are gone and the owner answers their own phone.

CThe first-5-seconds hook

The opener's only job is to buy you the next 20 seconds. The highest-converting move is the honest interrupt — name the elephant before they do and their defenses collapse:

The Honest InterruptREC
YOU"Hey Mike — full disclosure, this is a cold call. You can hang up, or give me 30 seconds and then decide. Fair?"
That "Fair?" earns a micro-yes, and a yes lowers resistance. You've removed the exact thing their brain was bracing for — so they relax. Never open with fake-warm "How are you today??"; it's the tell of every telemarketer.

EThe psychology of selling websites

Business owners do not buy websites. They buy more customers, more credibility, and not-losing to the competitor down the street. Every word lives in their world, never yours.

Your strongest weapon: the ROI frame

Anchor to one customer: "What's one new roofing job / patient / table booking worth to you?" When they say $400 or $8,000, the website stops being a cost — it's a machine that needs to produce ~2 of those to pay for itself. You're not selling a $2,500 site; you're selling a $400-job multiplier.

Loss aversion beats gain

"When someone's pipe bursts at 9pm and they google 'plumber in [town],' you don't show up — [competitor] does, and they call him" hits far harder than "a website will grow your business."

BThe 4 core objections — reflex responses

The universal rule: don't argue, ask a question that reframes. A statement invites a fight; a question makes them think. (Full bank of 16+ is on the Scripts page.)

OBJ 01"We already have a website."
Reframe: from "do you have one" → "is it actually working"
YOU"That's actually why I called — when's the last time that site brought you a customer? Do me a favor, pull it up on your phone right now. Does it look right? Most owners haven't seen their own site on a phone in years — and that's where 70% of your customers are looking."
OBJ 02"We don't need a website."
Reframe: make them picture the customer who walked away
YOU"Totally fair — a lot of owners feel that way. Quick question though: when someone hears about you and wants to check you out before they call, where do they look? Right — they google you. And right now what they find is [nothing / a half-empty Facebook page]. Your competitors are catching those people."
OBJ 03"We don't have the budget."
Budget objection = value not established. Back up, don't discount.
YOU"I hear you, and I'd never ask you to spend a dime on something that doesn't pay you back. So let me ask — what's one new job worth to you? If a site brought you even two of those a month, would the math work? That's all I'm trying to show you in those 15 minutes."
OBJ 04"Just send me an email."
90% a polite brush-off. Convert it to a micro-commitment.
YOU"Happy to — but real talk, you've got 40 emails you haven't opened and mine becomes 41. So I won't waste your time with that. Give me 10 minutes Thursday morning, I'll walk you through exactly what I found on a screen-share, and if it's not useful you never hear from me again. Does 9 or 11 work better?"

CHow to review recorded calls

Score every recorded call 1–5 on five things, written down:

  1. Tonality — calm and warm, or fast and nervous?
  2. Talk/listen ratio — you ~40%, them ~60%. If you're at 80%, you're pitching at them, not selling.
  3. Did you hit all 6 beats, or skip straight to pitching?
  4. Where exactly did you lose them? Timestamp it.
  5. Did you ask for the meeting — directly, with a specific time?

Track scores in a sheet. Look for the pattern across calls, not perfection on one. Most beginners lose at beat 5 — they pitch features and forget to ask for the meeting.

Compliance — not optional

Virginia is one-party consent, so you can record your own calls — but you'll dial other states, and several (CA, FL, IL, PA…) require all parties to consent. Safest universal habit: open recorded calls with "…and just so you know, I record my calls for training." It also makes you sound established. Scrub your list against the National DNC Registry; B2B-to-business-line calls have more latitude, but cell numbers and sole proprietors are a gray zone. Getting this wrong is expensive — confirm the current rules for the states you call.

DManaging fatigue & rejection

  • Track the input you control. Early on, your daily win is "100 dials," not "closed a deal." You can guarantee dials; you can't guarantee closes. Detaching progress from outcomes is the single biggest survival skill.
  • Rejection is statistics, not judgment. If 1 in 8 connects books, every "no" walks you toward the yes — you need the no's to get there. Some callers tally no's, because more no's = closer to the next deal.
  • Reset between calls. Stand up, shake it out for 5 seconds. Never carry the last call's energy into the next dial — the prospect hears a defeated voice in the first word.
  • Protect the streak. The danger zone is the day after a rough day, when you "don't feel like dialing." That's the day that decides whether you make it. Hit the number even if every call is garbage.
  • Weekly review, not daily spiraling. Variance is brutal day-to-day. Judge the week's ratios, not one bad afternoon.
Block 04 · The booked appointment

Inside the 15-Minute Meeting

The dial books the meeting. The meeting earns the money. This is the discovery-and-close conversation that turns "yes, 15 minutes" into money down — run it on a screen-share.

The golden rule of this call

Discovery is 70% of the win. Most beginners use the meeting to present. Elite closers use it to interrogate, gently — every number you pull out of their mouth becomes the price justification later. You don't sell the website. You make them describe the bleeding, then you describe the bandage.

↳The 6-phase structure

Disarm, set the map, get a micro-yes. They showed up half-expecting a hard sell — defuse it.
1–6m

Ask, don't tell. Find the pain, quantify it in their numbers. This is where the deal is won.
6–10m

Share screen. Show the gap — their Google result, their site on mobile, the competitor who outranks them.
10–11m

13–15m

1Re-frame & agenda

Open the meeting — disarm + mapREC
YOU"Mike, appreciate the 15 minutes. Here's how I'll use it so I don't waste yours: first I'll ask you a couple quick questions about how you get customers right now — because what I show you only matters if it fits your business. Then I'll share my screen and walk you through what I found and what your top competitor's doing. At the end, if it makes sense, I'll show you exactly what it'd take to fix it. If it doesn't, no hard feelings. Sound fair?"
THEM"Yeah, that works."
You just got a micro-yes to the whole structure, including "I'll show you what it'd take to fix it" — so when you name a price later, it's not a surprise ambush, it's the agenda they already agreed to.

2Discovery — the heart of the call

Ask, listen, write down the numbers. You're hunting two things: the pain (what's broken / what they're losing) and the value of a customer (so the price prices itself later). Talk 30%, let them talk 70%.

Discovery question sequenceREC
YOU"Before I show you anything — walk me through how you get customers right now."
YOU"Roughly what split is word-of-mouth versus people finding you online?"
YOU"What's one new [job / customer / patient] worth to you on average?"
NOTEWrite this number down. It is the entire pricing conversation.
YOU"And how many of those are you doing in a typical month right now?"
YOU"If I could reliably add even 3 to 5 of those a month, what would that do for you?"
YOU"Have you ever tried anything with your website or online before? How'd it go?"
YOU"Other than you, is anyone else part of a decision like this?"
NOTEFind the real decision-maker now — a hidden spouse/partner kills more closes than price does.
Every answer is ammunition. "A job's worth $600 and I do about 20 a month, mostly word of mouth, and yeah I'd love more" — you now know the exact ROI math and that online is a wide-open gap. You haven't pitched a thing yet and you've already won.
The pre-close (ask this before you reveal anything)

YOU ▸ "Last question before I show you — if I walk you through this and the fix makes sense, and the money works for what it brings you, is there any reason you wouldn't want to move forward?" If they say "no reason," you've removed their escape hatch. If they surface something ("I'd need to ask my wife"), you just found the real objection early — fix it now, not at the close.

3The reveal — show, don't tell

Share your screen. Walking them through it on screen lands 10× harder than describing it. The order:

  1. Their Google result — search "[their service] in [town]" live. Show that they're buried or missing while a competitor sits at the top.
  2. Their site on mobile — pull it up on a phone-width view. Show what breaks, what loads slow, what looks dated.
  3. The competitor, side-by-side — the one outranking them. "This is who's catching the customers you're not."
Narrating the revealREC
YOU"Okay, sharing my screen. So I searched '[plumber in town]' the way a customer would. Here's page one — and you're… not on it. This guy is. Now watch — here's your site on a phone. See how the menu's cut off and it took eight seconds to load? Most people leave after three. So even the folks who do find you are bouncing. That's the gap I called you about."
Let silence do the work after the reveal. Don't rush to rescue them. The discomfort of seeing it themselves is what creates urgency — you're a guide pointing at the wound, not a salesperson talking over it.

4Tie it to money

Convert findings to their ROIREC
YOU"So you told me a job's worth about $600 and you do roughly 20 a month, mostly word of mouth. Right now you're basically invisible to everyone googling at the exact moment they need a plumber — and that's the highest-intent customer there is, because they need it now. If we just captured a handful of those a month, that's a few thousand dollars in work you're currently handing to that other guy. Every single month."
You're using only the numbers they gave you. They can't argue with their own math. This is why discovery comes first — by now the price is going to feel small against the loss you just quantified.

5–6Present, price & close

Move straight into three options. Anchor high, recommend the middle, then go silent. The full pricing mechanics — tiers, recurring revenue, payment plans — live on the Pricing Playbook. The close itself:

Present price → assumptive closeREC
YOU"So I've got three ways to fix this. The Growth Engine — fully custom, built to dominate that search — runs around $8,000. Most owners in your spot go with the Lead Machine, which fixes everything we just looked at and is built to make the phone ring, at $3,500. And there's a lean Get-Found starter at $1,800 if you just want to stop the bleeding. For where you are, I'd put you on the Lead Machine."
NOTENow STOP. The first person to talk after the price loses. Let it sit, even if it's awkward.
THEM"…okay, yeah, the middle one makes sense."
YOU"Perfect — that's the one I'd have picked for you. To lock your spot in my build schedule I take a 50% deposit to start, balance on launch. I can get you a kickoff this week. Want to grab Thursday or would early next week be easier?"
Two moves close it: the alternative-choice ("Thursday or next week," never "do you want to?") and the deposit lock. A verbal yes is not a sale — money down plus a date on the calendar is. Always end on a scheduling question, never an open one.

↳Final objections at the table

"I need to think about it."

Isolate the real concern. YOU ▸ "Totally — what specifically do you want to think through? Is it the money, the timing, or whether it'll actually work?" Whatever they name is the only real objection. Handle that one.

"I need to talk to my partner."

Don't let it drift. YOU ▸ "Makes sense. Let's get the three of us on a quick 10-minute call so they can ask me anything directly — beats it getting lost in translation. Tomorrow or Friday?"

"Can you do it cheaper?"

Trade scope, never just discount. YOU ▸ "I can — if we trim it down. We could drop X and Y to land closer to your number. Want to do that, or keep it whole and split it into payments?"

"That's more than I expected."

Re-anchor to the loss. YOU ▸ "I get it. But you told me one job's worth $600 — this pays for itself at six jobs and then runs for years. We can also split it across three payments so it's easier on cash flow."

Block 05 · The money

Pricing Without Flinching

How to price web design for SMBs so the number lands soft, the middle tier sells itself, and you build recurring income — not a one-off invoice.

Market reality first

The numbers below are realistic mid-market SMB figures for a solo/small operator in the US — but they vary heavily by region, niche, and your portfolio. Early on, price toward the lower end to bank wins and testimonials; raise prices as your proof stacks. Treat these as anchors to adjust, not gospel.

AThe seven rules

  1. Anchor to ROI, never to hours. Price the outcome (more customers), not your labor. The second you price by the hour, you cap your income and invite them to scrutinize every minute.
  2. Always present three tiers. The high tier makes the middle look reasonable; the low tier gives the cautious somewhere to land. This single move — anchor, target, floor — is the biggest pricing lever you have.
  3. Recommend the middle. Don't make them choose in a vacuum. Tell them where you'd put them. Most will follow.
  4. Never lead with price. Lead with the ROI math you built in discovery. Price lands soft after value is established and hard before it.
  5. Break sticker shock into "cost per X." "$3,500 ÷ a $600 job = it pays for itself at six jobs, then runs for years."
  6. Never discount — trade scope. Dropping the price the moment they push teaches them the price was fake. If they need a lower number, remove something.
  7. Sell the build to get the client; sell the retainer to keep the income. Recurring revenue is the actual business (see C).

CThe care plan — your actual business

The build is a one-time cheque. The care plan is what makes this a real income. Attach a monthly plan to every client.

Care Plan · $75–300/mo (recurring)

Hosting, security, backups, monthly edits, and a simple "here's what your site did this month" report.

Pitching the care planREC
YOU"One more thing — a site isn't a 'build it and forget it.' It needs hosting, security, and updates, or it goes stale and slips down Google. My care plan handles all of that plus any edits you need, for $150 a month. Most clients take it so they never have to think about it. Want me to include it?"
Twenty clients on a $150/mo plan = $36,000/year of recurring income before you dial a single new prospect. This is what turns cold-calling from a hustle into a business.

DStructuring the payment

  • Deposit + milestone, never full upfront or full-on-delivery. Standard SMB terms: 50% to start, 50% on launch. The deposit is what actually locks the deal.
  • Payment plans dissolve budget objections without discounting. Splitting $3,500 into 3 × ~$1,200 changes the psychology entirely — same total, far smaller "ouch."
  • The deposit closes the sale. A verbal yes drifts. Card-on-file or invoice paid = committed. Always move to "let's get the deposit handled so I can lock your spot."

EPricing mistakes that kill beginners

✕ Pricing by the hour

Caps your income and invites a fight over every minute.

✕ Quoting one number

No anchor, feels arbitrary, trivially easy to reject.

✕ Softening the price

"It's only…" and nervous laughter tell them it's negotiable.

✕ Talking after the number

First to speak loses. State it, then shut up.

✕ Discounting at first push

Trains them that your real price is lower. Trade scope instead.

✕ Forgetting the retainer

You leave 80% of the lifetime value on the table.

YOU"It's right on your Google Business listing — which is actually what I called about. While I was on it I noticed [problem]…"
11"Is this a sales pitch?"
Own it → reframe to value
YOU"Eventually, sure — if it makes sense for you. But right now it's just me telling you about a problem on your listing that's sending customers to a competitor. You can do whatever you want with that."
12"I'll think about it."
Isolate the real concern
YOU"Of course — what specifically do you want to think through? The money, the timing, or whether it'll actually work? Let's knock that one out now so you're not chewing on it."
13"My nephew/kid builds sites."
Don't insult them → contrast outcomes
YOU"That's great, keep that relationship. Different thing though — is he building you something that actually shows up on Google and turns visitors into phone calls, or just something that looks nice? Those are two very different jobs. Worth a look either way."
14"All my business is word of mouth."
Validate → expose the leak
YOU"That's the best kind — and it's exactly why this matters. When someone refers you, the first thing that person does is google you to check you out. Right now what they find makes you look smaller than you are. You're losing warm referrals at the last step."
15"Just text me the info."
Convert to a tiny live commitment
YOU"Can do — but the whole point is showing you on screen, a text won't land it. Give me literally 10 minutes and you'll actually see the problem. I'll text you the calendar link right after — what's a good window tomorrow?"
16"We tried a website before, it didn't work."
Agree → separate 'a site' from 'a working site'
YOU"Yeah — most don't, because they're built to look pretty, not to get found and make the phone ring. Totally different approach. What'd you have before, and what were you hoping it'd do? Let me show you why it probably didn't and what actually moves the needle."

Discovery question bank

For the meeting — ask, write down numbersREC
YOU"Walk me through how you get customers right now."
YOU"What split is word-of-mouth versus people finding you online?"
YOU"What's one new [job/customer/patient] worth to you on average?"
YOU"How many of those do you do in a typical month?"
YOU"If I added 3–5 of those a month, what would that mean for you?"
YOU"Ever tried anything with your website before? How'd it go?"
YOU"A year from now, what's the number you're chasing?"
YOU"Other than you, who else is part of a decision like this?"
YOU"On a scale of 'this is fine' to 'this is costing me' — where's your website right now?"
The job-value number and the decision-maker question are the two that matter most. The first prices the deal; the second saves the close.

Closing lines

Alternative-choice

YOU ▸ "Want to grab Thursday, or is early next week easier?" Never "do you want to?" — give two yeses to choose between.

Assumptive

YOU ▸ "Perfect — I'll take a 50% deposit to lock your spot and we'll kick off this week." Act like it's already decided.

Summary close

YOU ▸ "So: you're invisible on Google, losing jobs to [competitor], a job's worth $600 to you — and this fixes all of it and pays for itself in six. Makes sense to start, right?"

Pre-close (de-risk early)

YOU ▸ "If the fix makes sense and the money works, any reason you wouldn't move forward?" Surfaces the real objection before you pitch.

The silence close

State the price. Then say nothing. First to talk loses. The pause does more work than any line you could add.

Deposit lock

YOU ▸ "Let's get the deposit handled so I can lock you into my build schedule — card, or you want an invoice?" A verbal yes isn't a close; money is.

Follow-up scripts

Mindset

"Send me an email" and "call me next quarter" are not dead ends — they're a pipeline. Most SMB closes land on the 2nd–4th touch. The caller who follows up systematically beats the one who only chases fresh leads.

Call-back after "send me an email"REC
YOU"Hey Mike — [Name], I sent that email Tuesday about your site. Didn't want it to get buried, so I figured I'd just grab you for two minutes. Got a sec?"
The email was always the setup for this call. Set a reminder the moment they ask for one.
The follow-up email (short)REC
YOUSubject: the thing costing you customers
YOU"Mike — quick recap of what I spotted: your site doesn't load on mobile and you're not showing up for '[service] in [town]', where [competitor] is #1. 15 minutes and I'll show you exactly what to fix. Here's my calendar: [link]. — [Name]"
Three sentences max. The email's job is to get the meeting, not to make the pitch.
No-show rescheduleREC
YOU"Hey Mike, [Name] — looks like we got our wires crossed for our call. No worries, happens. I blocked the time anyway, so I can do it now, or want to grab tomorrow morning instead?"
No guilt, no "you missed our meeting." Stay warm and make rebooking effortless.
"Circle back next quarter" callbackREC
YOU"Mike, [Name] — you told me to check back around now, so here I am, true to my word. Still want to fix that website situation, or has it sorted itself out?"
Showing up exactly when you said builds more trust than the pitch itself. Most callers never do.
"You went quiet" re-engageREC
YOU"Hey Mike — we talked a couple weeks back and you went a bit quiet, which usually means one of three things: it's not a priority, the timing's off, or something about the money felt off. Totally fine either way — just let me know which so I stop chasing the wrong thing."
Naming the three reasons makes it easy and low-pressure to reply honestly — and often restarts the deal.
